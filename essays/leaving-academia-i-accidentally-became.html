Leaving academia, I accidentally became a “freer” scientist.

During my PhD years, I often felt like I was trapped in a dream I couldn’t wake up from—grand, ambitious, but strangely unreal.

You spend years absorbing massive, intricate bodies of knowledge, yet you must follow the “approved paths” laid out by:

  • the invisible rules of your subfield

  • reviewers’ preferences

  • your lab’s inherited habits

  • the tastes of specific journals

All of these quietly decide what you’re allowed to do, how you’re supposed to write, and whether your ideas will ever be seen.

I used to think industry would simply replace these constraints with new ones—company strategy, stakeholders’ demands, team politics, budgets, timelines…

But once I actually began tackling a new real-world problem, I was shocked by something I’d never expected:

I didn’t have to care about disciplinary boundaries.
I didn’t have to worry about “stepping on” someone’s territory.
I didn’t have to ask whether a reviewer would approve.
I didn’t have to wonder which journal would accept it.

I could finally blend everything I had spent years learning—cognitive science theories, behavioral economics models, statistical inference—instinctively, like assembling a finely tuned sword.

And for the first time, the goal wasn’t a paper.
It wasn’t about pleasing reviewers.
It was simply: solve a real problem.

And the surprising part?
This integrated, intuition-driven approach actually solved it.

Even more unexpected: I met a manager who genuinely loves knowledge. A real PhD at heart. Sometimes we’ll casually flip through old, classic textbooks and talk about how different scientists approached the same question. Those quiet conversations—curiosity without pressure, learning without politics—felt more peaceful than most moments I had in academia.

No status games.
No publication anxiety.
Just the joy of ideas.

And somehow, this confirmed something I imagined more than a decade ago but never fully believed: Knowledge itself can be romantic.

Maybe that’s what this whole journey was always leading me toward.
